## Presentation Structure (20–25 min)

### Part 1: Introduction & Background *(3–4 min) — Speaker 1*

- **Hook:** Start with a paradox. *"70% of students have heard of IF, but 70% have never tried it. Why?"*
- **Topic:** Define IF (16:8, 5:2). Distinguish it from simple calorie cutting.
- **Relevance to HKU SPACE Students:** Link to the data (Figure 5). Students fear *"hunger affecting focus"* and *"unpredictable timetables."*
- **The Gap:** Social media tells them it works, but science says execution matters. Your goal: Perceptions vs. Reality.

---

### Part 2: Methodology & Demographics *(2–3 min) — Speaker 2*

- **Method:** Quantitative survey (N=27). Convenience sampling (Telegram/on-campus).
- **Who responded:** Show Figure 1 (Gender: 60% Male) and Figure 2 (Mostly Normal BMI).
- **Key Insight:** Your respondents are already health-conscious (Figure 3: many exercise 3–5x/week). This makes their fears about IF even more significant.

---

### Part 3: Key Findings – Benefits & Risks *(4–5 min) — Speaker 3*

- **Perceived Benefit (Data):** Q11 shows *"Weight loss"* is #1. Students believe it boosts focus.
- **The Contradiction (Science):** Cite Gudden et al. (2021) – No clear evidence of short-term cognitive benefits. Students are motivated by popularity, not proof.
- **Perceived Risks (Data):** Look at Column H (Q11). Low energy/fatigue and hunger are the top fears.
- **The Student Paradox:** They want weight loss but fear the fatigue will ruin their grades.

---

### Part 4: The Critical Problem – Knowledge Gaps *(4–5 min) — Speaker 4*

> *Focus on Section 2.5 of your report*

- **The Shocking Stat:** From your Excel (Row 1, Column AQ): **0 respondents** were fully certain what breaks a fast.
- **The Issue:** Students don't know if black coffee, diet soda, or milk breaks the fast.
- **The Consequence:** If they accidentally break the fast, they get no metabolic benefit, assume *"IF doesn't work,"* and quit.
- **Source of Problem:** Show Column G (Q6). Most info comes from **Social Media** (TikTok/Instagram), not healthcare professionals.

---

### Part 5: Barriers to Sustaining IF *(4–5 min) — Speaker 5*

- Show **Figure 4**: 18/30 chose *"Personal willpower."*
- Show **Figure 5**: *"Unpredictable timetable"* (9 votes) and *"Hunger affecting focus"* (7 votes) are the biggest barriers.
- **The Social Factor:** Q12 shows family/friends matter. Data shows students feel pressure to eat socially.
- **Conclusion:** IF fails not because it doesn't work, but because student life is chaotic and social.

---

### Part 6: Recommendations & Conclusion *(3–4 min) — Speaker 6*

- **Recommendation 1 (Procedural):** HKU SPACE should post clear guidelines near vending machines (e.g., *"Black coffee = OK. Latte = Breaks fast."*)
- **Recommendation 2 (Timetable):** Match eating windows to your actual class schedule. If you have a late class, shift your eating window later.
- **Recommendation 3 (Social):** Plan eating windows with friends to avoid pressure.
- **Final Takeaway:** IF is not a fad, but it is not a magic bullet. Without procedural knowledge, students will fail.

---

**Q:** *"Isn't IF just an eating disorder?"*

## SLIDE 3 — What is Intermittent Fasting? (Background)
**Speaker: Member 1**

**On the slide:**
- Simple definition box: *"Cycling between periods of eating and fasting"*
- Two common methods:
  - **16:8** – 16 hours fast, 8 hours eating
  - **5:2** – 5 normal days, 2 low-calorie days
- Key difference from traditional diets: Focuses on **WHEN** to eat, not **WHAT** to eat

**What to SAY:**
> *"Unlike traditional diets that restrict calories or food types, IF focuses on timing. The 16:8 method is the most popular among students. This flexibility appeals to busy people, but as we discovered, it also creates unique challenges for students."*

📖 *Citation: Aragon & Schoenfeld (2022)*
⏱ *~1 minute*

**What to SAY:**

Our survey confirmed that 'hunger affecting focus' is one of the top concerns. Understanding IF in our specific context is different from reading a clinical study."*

## SLIDE 9 — Where Do Students Get Information?
**Speaker: Member 3**

  - Friends/classmates – medium
  - Healthcare professionals – very small slice

Social media promotes simple, dramatic success stories. But precision nutrition says dietary advice must fit a person's unique body, lifestyle, and health needs. This mismatch is dangerous."*

## SLIDE 10 — The Critical Knowledge Gap: What Breaks a Fast?
**Speaker: Member 4**

**On the slide:**
- A dramatic visual: **0%**
- Text: *"Zero respondents were fully certain what substances break a fast"*
- Three common drinks:
  - ☕ Black coffee – ✅ OK
  - 🍵 Unsweetened tea – ✅ OK
  - 🥛 Milk or sweetened drink – ❌ Breaks fast

**What to SAY:**
> *"This is our most concerning finding. From our survey, not a single respondent was fully certain about what breaks a fast. Can you drink black coffee? What about a latte? Diet soda? Students do not know. If a student accidentally consumes calories during a fasting window, they lose the metabolic benefits. Then they assume 'IF doesn't work' and quit. This is not a failure of willpower. This is a failure of information."*

📖 *Citation: Ganesan et al. (2018) – insulin response and autophagy*
⏱ *~1.5 minutes*

## SLIDE 11 — Barriers to Sustaining IF: The Data

  - Friend factors: 2
- **Figure 5** – Biggest barriers as a student:
  - Unpredictable timetable: **9**
  - Hunger affecting focus: **7**
  - Social/family pressure: **7**

It is an unpredictable class schedule. Followed by hunger that affects studying. Followed by social pressure from family and friends. This tells us that IF fails not because students lack discipline, but because student life is fundamentally unpredictable."*

## SLIDE 12 — Summary of Key Findings
**Speaker: Member 5**

**On the slide:**
1. Students see IF as effective for weight loss but **fear energy loss**
2. **Social media** is the main information source, not professionals
3. **Zero** students fully understand what breaks a fast
4. **Unpredictable schedules** and social pressure are real barriers

**What to SAY:**
> *"To summarize: Students are interested in IF. But they get their information from social media, not science. They do not understand the basic rules of fasting. And even when they want to try, their class schedules and social lives get in the way. This creates a cycle of failed attempts and abandoned diets."*

## SLIDE 13 — Recommendation 1: Improve Nutritional Literacy
**Speaker: Member 6**

**On the slide:**
**Title:** What HKU SPACE Can Do

- 📋 Post clear guidelines near vending machines: *"Black coffee = OK. Latte = Breaks fast."*
- 📱 Promote trusted websites (not TikTok) for IF information
- 🍳 Emphasize protein-rich foods during eating windows (meat, fish, eggs)

**What to SAY:**
> *"Our first recommendation is for HKU SPACE to help students understand the basic rules of IF. Simple posters near vending machines could clarify what drinks are allowed during a fast. The college could also share links to evidence-based resources. Most students want to do IF correctly. They just don't know how."*

📖 *Citation: Ganesan et al. (2018)*
⏱ *~1 minute*

## SLIDE 14 — Recommendation 2: Adapt IF to Academic Life
**Speaker: Member 6**

**On the slide:**
**Title:** Student Strategies

- 🕐 Match eating windows to your actual lecture schedule
- 🍵 Use black coffee or green tea to manage hunger during study sessions
- 🥚 Prepare high-protein snacks (hard-boiled eggs, whole wheat sandwiches)
- 👥 Discuss fasting goals with friends/family to reduce social pressure

**What to SAY:**
> *"Second, students can adapt IF to their specific academic calendar. If you have a late class, shift your eating window later. Prepare portable protein snacks. And most importantly, talk to your friends and family. If they know your goals, they are less likely to pressure you to eat outside your window. IF must fit your life, not the other way around."*

📖 *Citation: Johnstone (2015)*
⏱ *~1 minute*
