## Presentation Speech: Australia's Social Media Ban for Under-16s – A Useful Measure or a Misguided Fix?

**Course:** CCSS4043 Global Issues & Everyday Life  
**Date:** 14 April 2026  
**Speakers:** Kwok Ho Yin, Tai Ngo Ting, Tam Chun Ho, Young Ho Tim, Zeng Chun Shing  
**HKU Space Community College**

### Speaker 1 (Slides 2-3: Opening Hook + Our Position + Global Reaction)

Hi everyone. Today we are talking about something that affects almost all of us: social media.

**Slide 2 – What Happened?**

Last year, Australia did something no country had ever done before. They passed a law that says: kids under 16 cannot use social media. Not with parent permission. Not for just one hour. Zero access.

The law started on 10 December 2025.

The government said they did this to protect young people from what they call "predatory algorithms" – those smart programs that learn what you like and keep feeding you more content to make you scroll forever. They also wanted to stop cyberbullying and harmful content.

But here's the thing. The law puts all the responsibility on the platforms – companies like Meta and TikTok – not on parents or kids. And if a ban replaces better internet rules, it becomes a misguided fix.

**Slide 3 – Our Position**

So what is our position? We say the ban is **conditionally useful**. That means it helps in some ways, but it fails in others.

**Why did this debate spread so fast?**

Because many governments around the world are watching. Countries like France, Denmark, and the UK are now thinking about doing the same thing. Supporters see the ban as a clear way to respond to public worry about youth mental health.

But tech companies are pushing back. They say the law is too simple, invades privacy, and is hard to enforce. Civil rights groups warn that it could cut off vulnerable young people from their online support networks.

So Australia's ban has become a test case. How far should governments go in controlling social media?

Let me pass to my teammate to explain some key ideas from our course.

### Speaker 2 (Slide 4: The Key Concepts from Lecture 6)

Thanks. Before we judge the ban, we need three simple ideas from Lecture 6.

**Idea one: Media convergence.**

This just means that a small number of big companies control most of what we watch, read, and share online. A few firms – like Meta, Google, and TikTok – shape what people see every day.

**Idea two: Disinformation and echo chambers.**

Algorithms – those smart computer programs – show you content that you already agree with. This creates what we call "echo chambers." You only hear your own voice bouncing back. This makes confirmation bias worse – you believe things that fit what you already think. And it spreads misinformation.

**Idea three: Culture war and governance.**

Governments can use AI-generated content and fake information to manipulate public opinion. This deepens the divide between different groups and makes culture wars worse online.

So why do these ideas matter for Australia's ban? Because social media is not neutral. It is controlled by big companies. It traps you in echo chambers. And it can be used to manipulate people.

Now let's look at what the law actually does.

---

### Speaker 3 (Slide 5: Case Overview – What Australia's Law Actually Does)

Hi. Let me explain exactly how the ban works.

**What platforms are included?**

The law targets major platforms. They must take "reasonable steps" to stop kids under 16 from having accounts.

**What are the penalties?**

If companies don't follow the law, they face fines up to $49.5 million Australian dollars.

**No parent opt-in.**

Unlike other countries, Australia's law does not let parents say "it's okay for my child." No exceptions.

**What is exempt?**

Messaging apps like WhatsApp, gaming platforms like Roblox, and education tools like Google Classroom and YouTube Kids are currently exempt. So kids can still use those.

**The immediate debate.**

Supporters say the ban protects youth mental health. Critics say it is a blunt instrument – too simple and crude – and it has serious privacy and enforcement problems.

So that is the law. Now let's look at why supporters think it helps.

---

### Speaker 4 (Slide 6: Why Supporters Think the Ban Helps)

Thanks. So why do people support this ban?

**The supporters' logic goes like this:**

Remove the account → Reduce algorithmic targeting → Cut exposure to harmful feeds → Give families breathing space.

**How does Lecture 6 connect to this?**

Lecture 6 describes major platforms as powerful digital intermediaries that shape what content we see. By restricting under-16 accounts, the ban reduces exposure to curated feeds that reinforce echo chambers and confirmation bias.

In other words, the ban acts as a preventive barrier. It blocks misinformation loops, hate speech, and online polarization before they start.

**But here is the main limitation.**

The policy reduces exposure, but it does not teach media literacy. It does not teach kids how to spot disinformation when they come back online at age 16.

So the ban helps in the short term, but it does not build long-term skills.

Now let me pass to my teammate to explain why critics say the ban backfires.

---

### Speaker 5 (Slide 7: Why Critics Say the Ban Backfires)

Thank you. So the ban sounds okay so far. But critics say it actually makes things worse. Here is why.

**First: Support networks matter.**

Vulnerable teenagers – including disabled youth and LGBTQIA+ kids – may lose spaces where they find identity, belonging, and emotional support. Lecture 6 teaches us that online spaces are not just risks. They are also places where culture and political voice are negotiated.

**Second: The governance problem remains.**

Young users will not just accept being blocked. They will shift toward VPNs to hide their location, fake identities, or smaller platforms with weaker moderation. These unregulated spaces have no safety rules.

So the law looks reactive, not systemic. It excludes users without actually fixing the platform environment that produces the harm.

Now let's look at the evidence.

---

### Speaker 1 (Back for Slide 8: Evidence Snapshot – Short-Term Wins, Serious Gaps)

Thanks. Let me give you the numbers.

**Short-term wins:**

- **4.7 million accounts** of suspected under-16s were removed in the early phase.
- **61% of parents** reported positive changes – better routines and more offline interaction.

**Serious gaps:**

- **61% of children aged 12 to 15** still had access to at least one restricted account. The ban did not catch them.
- **27% of parents** reported their children moved to underground or less regulated platforms.

**What does this mean?**

The ban may create immediate disruption. But uneven platform enforcement makes its long-term effectiveness much less clear.

So a ban alone is not enough. What else can we do?

---

### Speaker 2 (Back for Slide 9: Beyond Banning – Comparative and Complementary Approaches)

Thanks. Let me show you four different approaches and compare them.

**Approach 1: Total ban (what Australia did)**

- Main strength: Fastest way to reduce direct exposure to major platforms.
- Main weakness: Evasion, privacy concerns, loss of support networks.

**Approach 2: Safety-by-design**

- Main strength: Targets addictive defaults, algorithmic feeds, and privacy settings at the source.
- Main weakness: Harder to audit and enforce consistently.

**Approach 3: Digital literacy**

- Main strength: Builds long-term resilience, critical thinking, and healthier habits.
- Main weakness: Slow to show results and depends on school capacity.

**Approach 4: Hybrid policy**

- Main strength: Combines regulation, education, and age-appropriate design.
- Main weakness: Requires sustained coordination and political will.

So which is best? Let's find out.

---

### Speaker 3 (Back for Slide 10: Synthesis – Is the Ban Useful?)

Hi. So here is our synthesis – our final judgment.

**Conditional verdict:**

The ban is useful **only if** it is paired with media literacy, platform accountability, transparent enforcement, and broader internet governance reforms.

**Yes, in the short term:**

- Reduces immediate exposure to concentrated platform ecosystems
- Interrupts some misinformation and echo-chamber dynamics
- Forces platforms to take child safety more seriously

**No, in the long term:**

- Platforms are still powerful
- Enforcement is uneven
- Some young people lose valuable spaces for identity and support

So the ban is not a complete solution by itself.

### Speaker 4 (Back for Slide 11: Key Takeaways)

Thanks. Let me summarize the four key takeaways.

**Takeaway 1:**  
Lecture 6 helps us see the issue as a problem of platform power, disinformation, and internet governance – not just individual screen time.

**Takeaway 2:**  
Australia's ban may reduce short-term harm by limiting exposure to curated feeds and concentrated digital intermediaries.

**Takeaway 3:**  
The policy also creates costs: weak enforcement, workarounds, and reduced access to support networks for some vulnerable youth.

**Takeaway 4:**  
The strongest response is a hybrid model: platform regulation plus digital literacy, not prohibition alone.

Now let's conclude.

---

### Speaker 5 (Back for Slide 12: Conclusion)

Thank you. Here is our final conclusion.

Australia's experiment is a bold first step, but it is not a complete solution.

Lecture 6 helps explain why. The ban responds to misinformation, echo chambers, and concentrated digital power. But it does not fully answer the governance questions behind them.

**What should we do instead?**

Regulate platform design. Build digital literacy. Create age-appropriate online environments. Do not rely only on prohibition.

**Our final line:**

Protecting teens means shaping technology, not just blocking it.

## Q&A Simple Answers (Same as Before)

**Q: Isn't banning easier than teaching digital literacy?**

A: Easier doesn't mean better. Banning is quick but it fails in the long run. Teaching skills takes time but it works for life.

**Q: Shouldn't parents just control their kids' phones?**

A: Parents should help. But apps are designed by experts in addiction. No parent can fight that alone. That's why we need rules for the apps themselves.

**Q: So do you support the ban or not?**

A: We support it as a first step, but not as the only step. Ban without education is like locking the front door but leaving the window open. Kids will find a way in, and they won't have learned anything.
