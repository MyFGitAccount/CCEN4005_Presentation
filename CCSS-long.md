## Presentation Speech: Australia's Social Media Ban for Under-16s – A Useful Measure or a Misguided Fix?

**Course:** CCSS4043 Global Issues & Everyday Life  
**Date:** 14 April 2026  
**Speakers:** Kwok Ho Yin, Tai Ngo Ting, Tam Chun Ho, Young Ho Tim, Zeng Chun Shing

---

### Speaker 1 (Opening Hook – The Landmark Policy) – approx. 2 minutes

Hello everyone. Before we start, I want you to think about one question. When was the last time you went more than an hour without checking your phone? For many of us, it's hard to remember, right?

Now imagine someone told you that you cannot use social media at all until you turn 16. How would you feel? Angry? Isolated? Maybe even a little lost?

Well, that is exactly what is happening right now in Australia.

Last year, Australia became the first country in the world to ban anyone under the age of 16 from accessing social media. This law took effect on 10 December 2025. And it is a world-first. No other country has gone this far.

So what does the law actually say? It says that major platforms like TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, Facebook, YouTube, X – formerly Twitter – Reddit, and Twitch must take what the government calls "reasonable steps" to block users under 16. If they don't, they face fines of up to $50 million Australian dollars. That is about 33 million US dollars. So it is not a small penalty.

And here is something important. This law does not allow parents to opt their kids in. Even if a parent says "it's okay," the platform still has to block the child. That makes this ban much stricter than anything tried before in other countries.

Why did Australia do this? The government said they wanted to protect young people from what they call "predatory algorithms" – those smart computer programs that learn what you like and keep feeding you more content to keep you scrolling forever. They also wanted to reduce cyberbullying, harmful content about eating disorders or suicide, and the general decline in youth mental health.

Now, here is the interesting part. The ban is actually very popular with Australian parents. One poll found that 77% of Australians supported the law. But teenagers? They are angry. And experts around the world are divided.

Some countries like France, Denmark, the UK, and Malaysia are now considering their own "Australia-style" bans. But tech companies like Meta, which owns Facebook and Instagram, and Reddit are fighting back with legal challenges. In fact, Reddit filed a lawsuit in Australia's High Court calling the ban "legally erroneous."

So our question today is this: **Is Australia's social media ban a useful measure to protect young people, or is it a misguided fix that causes more harm than good?**

Our group's answer is that the ban is **conditionally useful**. It works in some ways but fails in others. And to understand why, we need to borrow a few simple ideas from Lecture 6.

Let me pass to my teammate to explain those ideas.

### Speaker 2 (Theoretical Framework – Key Concepts from Lecture 6) – approx. 2 minutes

Thanks. Before we judge the ban, we need to understand two key concepts from Lecture 6. Don't worry – I will explain them in plain English.

**First concept: Addictive affordances.**

That sounds like a mouthful, but it is actually very simple. "Affordances" are just the features of a technology. And "addictive affordances" are the features that are specifically designed to keep you hooked.

Think about it. Why does Instagram have infinite scroll? Why does TikTok autoplay the next video before you even decide? Why do you get push notifications that say "Your friend just posted"? None of this is an accident. These features are built by very smart engineers whose job is to keep your eyes on the screen for as long as possible. Because the longer you stay, the more ads you see, and the more money the company makes.

Lecture 6 calls this the "attention economy." Your attention is the product being bought and sold.

**Second concept: Surveillance capitalism.**

This also sounds complicated, but it is simple. You know how most social media apps are free? Well, they are not really free. You pay with your data.

Here is how it works. You sign up for a free account. Then the platform watches everything you do. What do you like? What do you share? How long do you look at a post? What do you search for? This is called surveillance or dataveillance.

Then the platform uses all that data to build a profile of you. It learns your fears, your desires, your political views, your insecurities. Then it feeds you content that keeps you engaged. And finally, it sells targeted advertising space based on your profile.

So in surveillance capitalism, you are not the customer. You are the product. Your attention and your data are what is being sold.

Now, here is why this matters for Australia's ban. When a child under 16 cannot create an account, the platform cannot track them. Cannot collect their data. Cannot feed them a personalized addictive algorithm. The ban breaks the entire attention economy loop before it even starts.

But – and this is a very important "but" – Lecture 6 also warns us about something else. It tells us that banning access does not teach young people how to handle the internet when they eventually get access. This is called **digital literacy**. It is the skill of knowing how to spot fake news, protect your privacy, recognize addictive design, and manage your screen time.

A ban without digital literacy is like putting a fence at the edge of a cliff but never teaching the child how to walk safely near cliffs in the future. Eventually, the fence will be removed, and the child will have no skills to protect themselves.

So with these ideas in mind – addictive affordances, surveillance capitalism, and digital literacy – let's look at what the ban actually does.

### Speaker 3 (Case Overview + Argument For the Ban) – approx. 2 minutes

Thanks. Let me give you a clear picture of how the ban works and why supporters think it is a good idea.

**First, which platforms are banned?**  
The law currently covers ten platforms: Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, X (formerly Twitter), YouTube, Reddit, Threads, Kick, and Twitch. Messaging apps like WhatsApp and gaming platforms like Roblox are exempt for now. YouTube Kids is also exempt because it is designed specifically for children.

**Second, how do platforms enforce the ban?**  
They have to take "reasonable steps" to figure out a user's age. This can include facial recognition scans, government ID checks like a driver's licence, or even behavior analysis – for example, looking at how long an account has existed and what kind of content the user posts. The law specifically says platforms cannot rely on users just telling the truth or parents vouching for their kids.

**Third, what happens if platforms fail?**  
They face fines up to $49.5 million Australian dollars. That is about 33 million US dollars. To put that in perspective, one expert said that Meta makes that much money in about two hours. So the fine is significant, but it is not going to bankrupt anyone.

Now, why do supporters think this ban is a good idea?

**Reason one: It removes the entire addiction machine.**  
Remember addictive affordances and surveillance capitalism? The ban cuts them off completely. No account means no tracking. No tracking means no personalized algorithm. No personalized algorithm means no endless doomscrolling loop. The ban does not just filter harmful content – it removes the structural engine of harm.

**Reason two: Early results show some success.**  
According to news reports from Reuters and the University of Melbourne, in the first month of the ban, platforms removed approximately 4.7 million accounts suspected of belonging to under-16s. That is a huge number. Also, a survey found that 61% of parents reported positive changes in their children – more face-to-face interaction with friends, better sleep routines, and less anxiety.

**Reason three: It forces platforms to take responsibility.**  
Before this law, social media companies mostly self-regulated. And self-regulation failed because their profit depends on keeping people hooked. The ban forces them to actually invest in age verification technology and child safety features.

So in the short term, the ban does what it is supposed to do. It reduces exposure to the most dangerous parts of social media for millions of young people.

But – and this is a big "but" – the ban has serious problems. Let me pass to my teammate to explain the other side.

### Speaker 4 (Argument Against the Ban – Unintended Consequences) – approx. 2.5 minutes

Thank you. So the ban sounds reasonable so far. But let me tell you why many experts, teenagers, and even some parents say it backfires. We are going to look at three major problems.

**Problem one: The ban hurts the most vulnerable kids the most.**

Think about a teenager with a physical disability. The Guardian newspaper interviewed a 15-year-old Australian boy named Ezra Sholl. Ezra is quadriplegic. That means he cannot move his arms or legs easily. He cannot just go out and meet friends at the mall or play sports. For him, social media is not entertainment. It is a lifeline. It is how he stays connected to the world. He told The Guardian that the ban would isolate him even further.

Now think about LGBTQIA+ teenagers living in small rural towns. In many of these towns, there are no support groups. There are no other kids who share their identity. The local community might even be hostile. For these teenagers, social media is often the *only* place where they can find acceptance, advice, and friendship. The ban takes that away.

So the ban claims to protect young people. But for the most vulnerable young people, it actually does the opposite. It cuts them off from their only support networks.

**Problem two: Kids find ways around the ban. And those ways are more dangerous.**

You think a determined teenager is going to accept being blocked? Of course not. They use VPNs – virtual private networks – to hide their location and pretend they are in a country without a ban. They use fake birthdays. They borrow older siblings' IDs. They create "ghost accounts" with false information.

Or they simply move to smaller, less regulated platforms. When the ban was announced, downloads of apps called Lemon8 and Yope surged. These apps are not yet on the government's restricted list. But they also have no real safety rules, no content moderation, and no age checks.

So what happens? The ban does not stop kids from using social media. It just pushes them into darker, more dangerous corners of the internet. That is a classic "second-order effect" – an unintended consequence that makes the original problem worse.

**Problem three: The ban does not teach any skills.**

A child who is banned from social media until 16 turns 16 one day. And on that day, they suddenly get full access to Instagram, TikTok, and Snapchat. But they have zero digital literacy. They have never learned how to recognize disinformation. They have never learned how to break free from addictive design. They have never learned how to protect their privacy.

The ban did not teach them any of that. It just postponed the problem. It is like keeping a kid out of the swimming pool until they are 16 and then throwing them into the deep end with no swimming lessons.

And here is the really bad news. Recent data from April 2026 – just a few weeks ago – shows that the ban is not even working properly. A report from the Molly Rose Foundation found that **61% of children aged 12 to 15 still have access to one or more of their restricted accounts**. Many of them did not even need VPNs. The platforms simply failed to identify and remove them.

Another survey found that 63% of parents believe the ban has failed. And about 27% of parents said their children have moved to underground platforms that are harder to monitor.

So the ban is not achieving its main goal. It is creating new problems. And it is not teaching young people how to be safe online.

So if a ban alone is not the answer, what else can we do?

### Speaker 5 (Comparative Approach + Synthesis + Conclusion) – approx. 2.5 minutes

That is exactly the right question. If banning kids is not enough, what is the alternative?

The answer from Lecture 6 and from multiple expert sources is this: **regulation plus education**. Let me explain what that means with concrete examples.

**First, regulate the platforms, not the kids.**

Instead of banning young people, force tech companies to make their products safer by default. For example, the UK's Online Safety Act requires platforms to remove illegal and harmful content or face massive fines. The European Union's Digital Services Act requires very large platforms to let independent researchers audit their algorithms.

What would this look like in practice? For users under 18, platforms could be required to:
- Turn off infinite scrolling
- Disable personalized "For You" feeds by default
- Set all accounts to private
- Block direct messages from strangers
- Remove push notifications during nighttime hours

This approach targets the source of the problem – addictive platform design – rather than punishing young users.

**Second, teach digital literacy in every school.**

Finland is often cited as a world leader in this area. They start teaching media literacy in kindergarten. By the time Finnish children are teenagers, they know how to spot fake news, how to verify sources, how to recognize propaganda, and how to manage their screen time. This builds lifelong resilience. A ban only lasts until age 16. Digital literacy lasts forever.

Why not combine both? Require schools to teach digital literacy as a mandatory subject, just like math or English. Teach kids how algorithms work. Teach them how to protect their privacy. Teach them how to break the addiction loop themselves.

**Third, include young people in the conversation.**

Right now, adults are making decisions about social media without asking teenagers what they actually need. Professor Pete Etchells from Bath Spa University said that young people have been "largely excluded from the debate." Professor Andrew Przybylski from Oxford said effective legislation must "include the voice of young people."

Imagine if the Australian government had held youth hearings before passing the ban. Imagine if they had asked teenagers: "What would actually help you?" They might have heard answers like: "Make platforms less addictive" or "Give us better reporting tools for cyberbullying" or "Teach us how to spot fake news." Instead, they just banned everyone.

**So, our final answer: Is the ban useful?**

Our group says: **Yes, but only conditionally.**

**In the short term, yes.** The ban removes the addictive loop for millions of kids. It forces platforms to take safety seriously. It gives families breathing space. Early data shows that 4.7 million underage accounts were removed, and 61% of parents saw positive behavioral changes.

**In the long term, no – not by itself.** The ban pushes kids into unregulated spaces. It isolates vulnerable youth like disabled teens and LGBTQIA+ kids in rural towns. It does not teach digital resilience. And enforcement is failing – 61% of kids still have access.

**Our final position:**

Australia's social media ban is a bold and well-intentioned first step. But it is not a complete solution. Protecting teenagers means shaping technology, not just blocking it.

We need a hybrid policy:
- Smart regulation that holds tech companies accountable for addictive design
- Mandatory digital literacy education in every school, starting young
- Age-appropriate design features like private defaults and limited notifications
- And real conversations with young people about what they actually need

A ban alone is like putting a bandage on a broken leg. It looks like you did something, but the real problem is still there. The real solution is to fix the platform, not just block the user.

Thank you for listening. We are happy to take any questions.

### Optional: Quick Q&A Anticipations

**Q: But isn't banning easier than teaching digital literacy?**  
A: Easier doesn't mean better. Banning is quick and looks strong. But it fails in the long run because kids turn 16 with no skills. Digital literacy takes time and money, but it works for life.

**Q: What about parents? Shouldn't they control their kids' phones?**  
A: Parents absolutely have a role. But platforms are designed by experts in addiction. No single parent can compete with that. That is why we need regulation – to make the platforms less addictive in the first place.

**Q: So do you support the ban or not?**  
A: We support it as a *first step*, but not as the only step. Ban without education is like locking a door but leaving the window open. Kids will find a way in, and they will not have learned anything.
